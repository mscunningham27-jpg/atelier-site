There is a question most Christian artists carry quietly, and almost none of them ask out loud.

Not the question about whether their work is good enough — that one gets asked constantly, sometimes in the small hours of the morning. Not the question about whether they are wasting their life — that one surfaces during every slow season. The question underneath both of those, the one that tends to stay buried, is this:

*Is it wrong that I care so much about beauty?*

The hunger is real. The desire to make something that stops a person, that earns a second look, that holds together the way a good sentence holds — it is not a small thing. And for a certain kind of Christian artist, the hunger comes with a low-grade guilt attached. Wanting your work to be beautiful feels uncomfortably close to wanting your work to be noticed. Which feels uncomfortably close to pride.

## Where the guilt comes from

The suspicion has a history. It shows up in the long Protestant anxiety about art and ornamentation — the fear that beauty pulls attention toward itself and away from God. It shows up in the practical wing of evangelical culture, which tends to evaluate work by its utility: does it save souls? does it make disciples? A painting that is merely beautiful, without a gospel message printed alongside it, has always had a slightly uneasy status in that frame.

And there is a real danger buried in the suspicion. Vanity is a real thing. The desire for acclaim can corrupt a creative life, slowly and without announcing itself. The artists who have written most honestly about this — Flannery O'Connor, Makoto Fujimura, the psalms themselves — all know it.

But there is a difference between the corruption of a desire and the desire itself.

## What beauty actually is

Wanting your work to be beautiful is not the same as wanting your work to be praised. The distinction matters enormously, and it tends to get lost.

Beauty is not a style or a category. It is not the same as prettiness, or pleasantness, or the kind of visual comfort that makes a room easy to be in. Beauty, in the theological sense — the sense that Augustine meant when he wrote *our heart is restless until it rests in Thee* — is about rightness. About a thing being what it is, fully and without apology. About form and meaning and function arriving at the same place at the same time.

The hunger to make something beautiful is, at its root, a hunger for that rightness. And that hunger is not foreign to the faith. It is written into it.

> "He has made everything beautiful in its time." — Ecclesiastes 3:11

The Maker of all things is described, throughout Scripture, as someone who cares about the beauty of what he makes. The tabernacle specifications in Exodus are embarrassingly specific — cubits and colors and the precise sheen of woven fabric. The temple that Solomon built was not merely functional. The psalms are poems, with meter and image and the kind of attention to form that belongs to art, not only to prayer.

## The reframe that changes everything

The question is not whether your desire for beauty is vanity. The question is what you are pointing the beauty toward.

A work can be beautiful and self-serving. A work can also be beautiful and utterly given away — made with everything you have, for Someone other than yourself, offered as the kind of thing that makes a person say *there is something more going on here than I can name.*

Wanting your work to be beautiful is not vanity. It is, at its best, a form of faithfulness. It is the refusal to offer something half-made. It is the recognition that what you set before God and before the people God loves is worth your full attention.

The guilt is understandable. But it is not required.

If anything, the question worth sitting with is not *is it wrong to want my work to be beautiful?* It is a harder and better one:

*Am I making it beautiful enough?*
